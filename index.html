Australia's products are currently demanded by many nations, particularly those in Asia led by China (Marks, 2020). Statistics show that China purchased commodities from Australia worth more than $116 billion in 2019, with iron ore being the main component. The openness rate—the proportion of imports and exports to GDP, has consistently risen in recent years as Australia has continued to optimize and reform its trading patterns and regulations. Marks (2020) has argued that Australia holds a privileged position in international trade due to its resource advantages and considerable demand.

The comparison reveals that over the past 20 years, Australia's import demand has increased significantly in some countries. As an illustration, in 2000, just 5.4% of Australia's total exports went to China; by 2019, that percentage had increased to 38.7%. However, demand for Australian exports has gradually decreased in other nations. For instance, in 2000, the proportion of Australia's total exports to Japan was 19.8%; in 2019, that proportion dropped to 14.8%. International politics, national policy, and diplomatic maneuvers frequently influence changes in international trade, with mutual benefit and win-win outcomes remaining an ongoing goal for all nations (Tan, 2021).

To compare imports and exports with Australia, the 20 trading partners who cooperate the most were chosen. There is an obvious pattern of trade between countries and Australia: imports and exports are positively correlated, the more commodities imported into Australia, the more exports to Australia. Among these partner countries, China, Japan and Korea are the three countries having the highest trade flows with Australia.
